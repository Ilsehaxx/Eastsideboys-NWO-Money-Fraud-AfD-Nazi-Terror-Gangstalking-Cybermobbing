Art Spiegelman is the author of "Maus," a groundbreaking graphic novel that depicts the Holocaust through anthropomorphic animal metaphors. In the story, Jews are portrayed as mice, Nazis as cats, Poles as pigs, and Americans as dogs. Published in two volumes (1986 and 1991), it chronicles the experiences of Spiegelman's father, Vladek, a Polish Jew who survived Auschwitz and other concentration camps.

"Maus" won the Pulitzer Prize in 1992 and is widely regarded as a seminal work in graphic literature and Holocaust remembrance. It humanizes the horrors of the Nazi genocide while exploring themes of memory, guilt, and intergenerational trauma.

The novel's title, "Maus" (German for "mouse"), directly references the animal allegory used to represent the persecution of Jews. Spiegelman's innovative narrative style blends comics with oral history, making the Holocaust accessible to new generations.

Connections to Symbol Misuse:
- Svastika: The ancient Hindu symbol of prosperity, appropriated by Nazis as their emblem, misrepresenting its peaceful origins.
- The Roman: Refers to the Roman salute (braccio teso), adopted by Italian fascists under Mussolini and later by Nazis, symbolizing authoritarian revival.

"Maus" serves as a powerful reminder of how symbols and histories are twisted, underscoring the importance of combating cultural appropriation and radicalization.
